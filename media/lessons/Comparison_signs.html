In daily life, we can compare things in many ways.

For example, here are a couple of ways we can compare two similar things:

  • Alice's hair is darker than Bob's hair.
  • Charlie's room is cleaner than Diana's room.
  • Ernie's car is bigger than Fred's car.
Notice how in each example, we are using words in italics to compare similar things (hair, rooms, and cars to be specific).

We can do similar things with numbers! You might be thinking that a number cannot be 'darker' or 'cleaner' than another number, and you would be correct. The only way numbers can be compared is by comparing their amounts.

Here are some examples where we compare two numbers:
  • Two is greater than one.
  • Four is less than nine.
  • Five is equal to five.

An important thing to keep in mind is that when we compare two numbers, there are only three possible outcomes:
  1. The first number is greater than the second number. For example, 3 is greater than 0.
  2. The first number is less than the second number. For example, 1 is less than 5.
  3. The first number is equal to the second number. For example, 6 is equal to 6.
Because typing out sentences like these takes too much work, we have invented the following three signs to make it easier to represent these ideas:

Sign Name of Sign
\(>\) Greater-than sign
\(<\) Less-than sign
\(=\) Equals sign
The greater-than sign (\( > \)) is used when the number on the left is greater than the number on the right.
For example, we write '3 is greater than 0' as \( 3>0 \) using the greater-than sign.

The less-than sign (\( < \)) is used when the number on the left is less than the number on the right.
For example, we write '1 is less than 5' as \( 1<5 \) using the less-than sign.

The equals sign (\( = \)) is used when the number on the left is the same as the number on the right.
For example, we write '6 is equal to 6' as \( 6=6 \) using the equals sign.

This little trick might help you tell the greater-than and less-than signs apart: The pointy end of the sign always points to the smaller number.
In the first example \( 3>0 \), 3 is bigger than 0, so the pointy end points to the 0. In the second example \( 1<5 \), 1 is smaller than 5, so the pointy end points to the 1.
