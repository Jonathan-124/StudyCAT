We can extend the idea of multiplication and division to negative numbers, and do the computations easily with one handy trick:

  1. Multiply/divide the two numbers, ignoring whether they're positive or negative
    • If the two numbers being multiplied/divided have the same sign (in other words, either both are positive numbers or both are negative numbers) — the result is a positive number
    • If the two numbers being multiplied/divided have different signs (in other words, one is a positive number and the other is a negative number) — the result is a negative number
Here's an example: Calculate \(3 \times \scriptsize - \normalsize 2\). First, we multiply the numbers while ignoring whether they're positive or negative to get \(3 \times 2 = 6\). Because the two numbers have different signs (3 is positive and -2 is negative), the answer is a negative number. \(3 \times \scriptsize - \normalsize 2 = \scriptsize - \normalsize 6\).

Exercise 1

Calculate \(\scriptsize - \normalsize 12 \div 3\)


Show solution
The answer is \(-4\).

First, we divide the numbers while ignoring whether they're positive or negative: \(12 \div 3 = 4\). Because the two numbers have different signs (12 is positive and -3 is negative), the answer is a negative number. \(\scriptsize - \normalsize 12 \div 3 = \scriptsize - \normalsize 4\).

Exercise 2

Calculate \(\scriptsize - \normalsize 2 \times \scriptsize - \normalsize 6\)


Show solution
The answer is \(12\).

First, we multiply the numbers while ignoring whether they're positive or negative: \(2 \times 6 = 12\). Because the two numbers have the same sign (-2 is negative and -6 is negative), the answer is a positive number. \(\scriptsize - \normalsize 2 \times \scriptsize - \normalsize 6 = 12\).

Show solution
The answer is \(-12\).

First, we divide the numbers while ignoring whether they're positive or negative: \(132 \div 11 = 12\). Because the two numbers have different signs (-132 is negative and 11 is positive), the answer is a negative number. \(\scriptsize - \normalsize 132 \div 11 = \scriptsize - \normalsize 12\).
