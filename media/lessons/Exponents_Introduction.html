Recall that one way multiplication is defined is a way to denote a number added to itself many times. For example, computing \( 5+5+5+5+5+5 \) is the same as computing \( 5 \times 6 \). This begs the question, is there a simple way to denote a number multiplied by itself many times?

Exponents (also known as powers) are used to denote a number multiplied by itself many times. The number that is being multiplied is called the base, while the number of times it is multiplied to itself is called the exponent. The exponent is always written on the top-right of the base as a small number.

For example, \( 5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5\) is a number multiplied by itself, so this expression can be written as an exponent. The base is the number being multiplied, which is 5 in this case. The exponent is the number of times it is being multiplied, which is 6 in this example. The same expression written as an exponent becomes \(5^6\), where 5 is the base and 6 is the exponent.

Exercise 1

Write \(4 \times 4 \times 4\) as an exponent expression.


Show solution
The correct answer is \( 4^3 \).

The base is the number that is being multiplied to itself, which is 4 in this case. It is being multiplied to itself 3 times, so 3 is the exponent. The exponent is always written on the top-right of the base as a small number, so \(4 \times 4 \times 4 = 4^3\).


Exercise 2

Calculate \(9^2\).


Show solution
The correct answer is \( 81 \).

\(9^2 = 9 \times 9 = 81\).


When describing an exponent using words, we usually say "{base} to the power of {exponent}". For example, putting \(2^3=8\) into words, we say "2 to the power of 3 equals 8".

Another way to describe an exponent with an exponent of 2 or 3 is to say "{base} squared" or "{base} cubed" respectively. For example, "3 squared" means \(3^2\), while "2 cubed" means \(2^3\).
