Let's start with a question. How many squares are there below? $$ \begin{matrix} &\square&\square&\square&\square&\square&\square&\square \\ &\square&\square&\square&\square&\square&\square&\square \\ &\square&\square&\square&\square&\square&\square&\square \\ &\square&\square&\square&\square&\square&\square&\square \\ &\square&\square&\square&\square&\square&\square&\square \\ &\square&\square&\square&\square&\square&\square&\square \\ \end{matrix} $$ One way to do this is to count the squares one by one, and we can figure out that there are 42 squares in total. However, this is a very inefficient way of solving the problem. Even for such a short problem, it already takes a lot of time to count the number of squares.

You might notice that the squares are arranged very nicely. There are 7 columns in total, and each column has exactly 6 squares. A slightly simpler way to count the total number of squares is, instead of counting one square at a time, we count one column at a time! In this example, each column has 6 squares, and since there are 7 columns in total, we count by six seven times (\( 6+6+6+6+6+6+6 \)) to get the answer of 42!

There is an even simpler way to represent the addition of a number to itself, over and over again â€” multiplication! To rephrase what we did in the example more simply, we could say '6 multiplied by 7', which is another way to say 'add 6 to itself 7 times'.

Because writing 'multiply' becomes tiresome after a while, we use the symbol \( \times \) (called a multiplication sign) instead to write it as $$ 6 \times 7 $$ Since the result of \( 6 \times 7 \) is equal to 42, we can also write $$ 6 \times 7 = 42 $$ The result of multiplication (the number on the right of the equals sign) is also called the product. In this example, we say the product of 6 and 7 is 42.

Notice that in this example, you can also count the total number of squares one row at a time instead of counting one column at a time!
There are 7 squares in each row, and 6 rows in total. Adding 7 to itself 6 times (\(7+7+7+7+7+7\)) is the same as saying 7 multiplied by 6 (\( 7 \times 6 =42 \)), which also gives us the total number of squares.

In other words, order of multiplication does not matter. You can always switch the order of the two numbers that you are multiplying, and still get the same product.

Let's go through some exercises.

Exercise 1

Fill in the blank. $$ 3 \times 8 = \square$$


Show solution
The correct answer is \( 3 \times 8 = 24 \).

We add 3 to itself 8 times, and get the product of 24. Recall that since order of multiplication does not matter, we can add 8 to itself 3 times (\( 8 \times 3 \)) and get the same answer.


Exercise 2

Charlie has 4 boxes, and each box has 6 cookies. How many cookies does charlie have in total?


Show solution
The correct answer is 24.

To find the total number of cookies Charlie has, we need to add the number of cookies in each box to itself 4 times. \( 6 \times 4 = 24\), so there are 24 cookies in total.


A final useful concept that you might have encountered is multiples. A multiple of a number is any number that is the product of the original number multiplied by another.
For example, 3, 6, 9 are all multiples of 3, as \(3 \times 1=3\), \(3 \times 2=6\), and \(3 \times 3=9\). 4 is not a multiple of 3, as there is no number that can go in the blank \(3 \times \square =4\).

Exercise 3

Which of the following is not a multiple of 2?


Show solution
The correct answer is \( 1 \).

\( 2 \times 1=2\), \( 2 \times 2=4\), and \( 2 \times 4=8\). Since there is no number to put in the blank in \( 2 \times \square=1\), 1 is not a multple of 2.
To summarize multiplication:
  • When adding the same number to itself many times, we use multiplication
  • Multiplication is represented by the Multiplication sign (\( \times \))
  • The result of multiplication is called the product
  • Order of multiplication does not matter
  • A multiple of a number is any number that is the result of the original number multiplied by another
